Do

  • Always maintain clear space equal to the height of the "n3" letterform around the logo on all sides.
  • Use the logo on approved background colors only — n3 Red, Black (#0A0A0A), Dark (#111111), or White.
  • Maintain a minimum display size of 80px wide to preserve legibility of the "3" numeral.

Don't

  • Don't stretch, skew, or distort the logo proportions in any direction.
  • Don't recolor the logo — no gradients, tints, or off-brand colors. Use only approved variants.
  • Don't add drop shadows, glows, outlines, or decorative effects to the logo mark.

Color Usage

Primary actions

Use n3 Red (#E31337) for CTAs, active states, links, and the primary brand mark. Never use it for large background fills in UI contexts — it draws the eye and should signal action.

Depth & layering

The four dark tones (#0A0A0A, #111111, #1A1A1A, #2A2A2A) create depth. Stack them: page background → section → card → border. Never skip a step.

Text hierarchy

#E5E5E5 for primary text, #888888 for secondary/muted, pure white (#FFFFFF) only over the red brand color. Avoid pure white on dark — it's harsher than necessary.

04

Brand Voice

How n3ware speaks. Consistent tone builds trust faster than any visual asset.

Technical but accessible

We speak the language of builders, but we never lose the client. Clear, direct, jargon-free when it counts. A developer gets it. A bakery owner gets it too.

Bold and direct

No fluff. No filler. n3ware does what it says. Our copy leads with the result, not the feature. Tell people what changes for them — not how the sausage gets made.

Builder-first

We respect the developer. We celebrate the business owner who tries to build. Meet both where they are — no condescension for the non-technical, no hand-holding for the experienced.

Tone in Practice

Don't write this

"Leverage our comprehensive suite of visual editing tools to optimize your web presence and drive digital engagement across your customer touchpoints."

Write this

"Edit any website. Visually. One script tag."

Start with the result

Lead with what the user gets, not the technology that enables it. Outcome first, mechanism second (if at all).

Cut 30% of every sentence

Every first draft has words that add length without meaning. Find them. Delete them. Then delete 10% more.

Avoid adverbs and intensifiers

"Incredibly powerful," "extremely fast," "truly seamless" — these weaken the claim. Trust the noun and verb.
